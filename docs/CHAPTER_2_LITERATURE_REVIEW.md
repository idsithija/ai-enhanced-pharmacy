# Chapter 2: Literature Review

## 2.1 Overview

This chapter examines existing pharmacy management systems, particularly those deployed in Sri Lankan pharmacies, and identifies critical gaps that modern AI-powered solutions can address. The review draws from field research conducted across three community pharmacies and analysis of current market offerings.

## 2.2 Existing Pharmacy Management Systems in Sri Lanka

The Sri Lankan pharmacy sector currently relies on several established systems including PharmaSoft, MediTrack, and ChemistPro. These systems provide basic functionalities such as inventory tracking, billing, and sales records. According to field observations from three pharmacies visited between 2024-2025, the majority utilize locally developed software solutions that focus primarily on transaction processing and basic stock management.

PharmaSoft, one of the widely adopted systems in Sri Lanka, offers point-of-sale integration, basic inventory management, and customer billing features. However, the system operates entirely on manual data entry, requiring pharmacists to type prescription details manually into the system. MediTrack similarly provides inventory control and sales reporting but lacks predictive analytics or automated alerts. ChemistPro, another popular choice among small to medium pharmacies, offers basic expiry date tracking but requires manual daily checks rather than providing proactive notifications.

## 2.3 Field Research Findings

Primary research conducted through a pharmacy professional with three years of experience across three different pharmacies in Sri Lanka revealed consistent pain points. All three establishments utilized commercial pharmacy management software, yet several critical operational challenges persisted.

The most significant issue identified was the complete absence of artificial intelligence integration. Pharmacists spent considerable time manually entering prescription data from handwritten or printed prescriptions, a process prone to human error and time-consuming during peak hours. One pharmacy reported that prescription entry could take 2-5 minutes per customer during busy periods, creating bottlenecks and customer dissatisfaction.

Inventory management presented another major challenge. While existing systems stored expiry dates, they lacked proactive notification mechanisms. Staff had to manually run reports or physically check stock to identify expiring medications. This reactive approach often resulted in expired stock being discovered too late, leading to financial losses. One pharmacy estimated losing approximately LKR 50,000-100,000 monthly due to undetected expiring inventory.

Stock-level monitoring proved equally problematic. Systems displayed current quantities but did not predict future demand or provide intelligent reordering suggestions. Pharmacies frequently experienced either stockouts of high-demand medications or oversupply of slow-moving items. The absence of demand forecasting meant that purchasing decisions relied entirely on staff intuition rather than data-driven insights.

## 2.4 Limitations of Current Systems

Analysis of existing pharmacy management systems reveals several fundamental limitations. Traditional systems lack optical character recognition capabilities, forcing manual transcription of all prescription information. This not only consumes valuable staff time but introduces transcription errors that can have serious consequences in pharmaceutical dispensing.

Conventional inventory management operates on threshold-based alerts at best, lacking sophisticated algorithms that account for consumption patterns, seasonal variations, or expiration timelines. The systems store data but do not analyze it, missing opportunities to optimize stock levels and reduce waste. Modern pharmacies generate substantial transaction data, yet existing solutions provide only basic reporting rather than actionable predictive insights.

Security implementations in current systems often rely on simple username-password combinations without multi-factor authentication or role-based access controls. Data encryption, if present, typically covers only transmission rather than storage. This leaves sensitive patient and business information vulnerable, particularly concerning given the increasing regulatory focus on healthcare data protection.

## 2.5 Research Gap and Proposed Solution

The literature and field research clearly identify a significant gap between current pharmacy management capabilities and modern technological possibilities. While international markets have seen emergence of AI-powered healthcare solutions, Sri Lankan pharmacies continue operating with systems designed primarily for transaction recording rather than intelligent assistance.

The proposed Intelligent Pharmacy Management System addresses these gaps through three core innovations. First, OCR-powered prescription scanning eliminates manual data entry by automatically extracting medication names, dosages, and instructions from prescription images. This reduces processing time from minutes to seconds while minimizing transcription errors. Second, intelligent inventory management combines real-time tracking with predictive analytics, providing automated alerts for expiring stock and forecasting future demand based on historical patterns. Third, the system employs modern security standards including JWT authentication, role-based access control, and comprehensive data encryption.

Unlike existing solutions that simply digitize manual processes, this system leverages artificial intelligence to transform pharmacy operations. The OCR functionality represents a fundamental shift from reactive data entry to proactive information capture, while demand prediction moves inventory management from intuition-based to data-driven decision making.

## 2.6 Summary

Existing pharmacy management systems in Sri Lanka, while functional for basic operations, lack the intelligence and automation that modern technology enables. Field research confirms that pharmacies face daily challenges with manual prescription processing, reactive inventory management, and limited analytical capabilities. The proposed solution fills this gap by integrating AI-driven features specifically designed to address the documented pain points, offering Sri Lankan pharmacies access to capabilities previously available only in expensive international systems.
